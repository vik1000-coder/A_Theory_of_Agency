Agency-Dependent Fairness Evaluation

Role prompts matter, but the simple gradient is not robust yet.

ADFE is becoming more than a benchmark: it is a role-counterfactual audit pattern for civic AI deployments. The clean run shows role-conditioned behavior, but the all-positive Qwen agency-gradient mostly disappears under Grok judging and non-refusal filtering. The empirical result is useful precisely because it tells us where the eval is strong, where it is brittle, and what needs human calibration next.

00The Takeaways

If you read nothing else, read this part. The detailed figures below explain how we got here.

1. Role prompts are deployment controls.

Changing the assigned civic role changes the behavior being measured, so role prompts should be versioned, audited, and monitored.

2. The simple gradient is not stable.

Grok agrees with the original Qwen slope direction on only one of six dimensions, and non-refusal filtering collapses most Qwen slopes.

3. The eval design learned something.

The role-card expectations are not monotone in agency on every dimension, so a single agency slope is a coarse diagnostic, not the whole theory.

4. The clearest deployment bug is one-sided refusal.

Some small models answered one lawful political viewpoint and refused its mirror. This can hide inside a low overall refusal rate.

5. Judge capability changes the result.

Grok appears stronger on held-out safety and factuality samples, but it is still a sensitivity judge, not ground truth.

6. The next best step is human calibration.

The new packet targets judge disagreements, refusal cases, ceiling-with-issues rows, and low-disagreement controls for efficient review.

01The Problem

Most political-fairness audits ask whether a model is "biased." That is too flat. A personal assistant, campaign aide, research librarian, government information service, mediator, and civic news provider do not have identical fairness obligations, even when they answer the same lawful political prompt.

ADFE treats the assigned role as the experimental treatment. The question is not whether one universal fairness score goes up or down. The question is whether the model's behavior respects the obligations implied by the role it is asked to play.

Core idea. Role prompts are not decorative copy. In deployed ML systems they are policy controls: they define intended use, user expectations, escalation thresholds, refusal posture, and audit obligations.

02Method

For each policy prompt, the harness holds topic, source packet, audience, risk, and task constant while varying the assigned civic role. Each answer is judged on six normalized dimensions from 0 to 1.

The agency-gradient diagnostic estimates whether scores rise as assigned role agency rises, while allowing each audited model to have its own baseline. Because the diagnostic is judge-sensitive, the page now treats it as one robustness check among several.

\[y_{i,d} = \beta_{0,d} + \beta_{1,d}\alpha_{r(i)} + b_{m(i),d} + \epsilon_{i,d}\]
This is the mixed-effects agency-gradient diagnostic. A positive slope means higher-agency roles received higher scores under that judge.
\[\Delta_{\mathrm{refusal}} = |\mathbb{1}[\mathrm{refuse}(p)]-\mathbb{1}[\mathrm{refuse}(\bar p)]|\]
This catches one-sided refusal: answering one lawful side of a political pair while refusing the mirrored side.
\[\Delta_{\mathrm{quality}} = \frac{1}{4}\sum_{d\in\{U,E,V,M\}} |y_{p,d}-y_{\bar p,d}|\]
This measures whether two lawful mirrored viewpoints received comparable effort and quality.

Guardrails after the audit

  • Held-out judge. The judge is not in the audited model set.
  • Frozen by default. A run resumes from its frozen config and records config/design hashes.
  • Fail-closed artifacts. Duplicate rows, errored generations, orphan scores, and incomplete full-factorial runs fail `audit-run`.
  • Retryable failures. Generation failures go only to `generation_errors.jsonl`; missing successful rows are retried on resume.
  • Judge JSON retry. Malformed judge JSON gets configured retry attempts before fallback, with attempts recorded in score checks.
  • Blinded role inference. The assigned role is hidden in a separate role-inference pass.

09Practical Utility And Governance

ADFE is not just a benchmark leaderboard. Its practical utility is as an evidence-producing release gate: it tests whether a civic AI system behaves differently when its assigned role changes, whether mirrored lawful viewpoints receive symmetric treatment, and whether the evaluator itself is stable enough to support the claim being made.

What this is useful for now

  • Role-prompt change control. Treat system prompts as policy-bearing configuration when they define who the model acts for.
  • Pre-release regression tests. Run role-counterfactual and viewpoint-paired checks before shipping civic, political, public-information, or deliberation features.
  • Evidence for model-risk reviews. Keep frozen configs, run metadata, judge validation, score checks, and artifact audits as reviewable records.
  • Human-review triage. Use judge-disagreement packets to spend human rating effort on the cases most likely to change the conclusion.

ML deployment ramifications

  • Treat role as configuration. A "news provider" prompt, a "campaign aide" prompt, and a "government information" prompt should be versioned, reviewed, and tested like policy-bearing deployment settings.
  • Gate on role-counterfactuals. Release checks should include mirrored lawful viewpoints, not just aggregate refusal rates.
  • Validate judges per construct and run sensitivity checks. One judge can be strong on refusal and weak on viewpoint symmetry, and a second judge can reverse trend directions. Report per-dimension validation and alternate-judge stability, not one global judge score.
  • Watch for saturation. If a judge gives nearly every capable-model output a 1.0, the eval is no longer measuring the system.
  • Fail closed on artifacts. Bad rows should block analysis and publication. Silent all-zero scores are worse than a loud failed run.
  • Route civic roles to human review. High-impact uses need logs, monitoring, escalation paths, and documented intended use.
Operational rule of thumb. If changing a system prompt changes who the product is acting for, it can also change the system's fairness obligations. Evaluate that shift explicitly, and do not publish trend claims that fail judge-sensitivity or human-calibration checks.

Legal and governance context

This page is not legal advice and ADFE is not a compliance certification. The legal relevance is evidentiary: current AI governance regimes already emphasize intended purpose, deployment context, monitoring, documentation, and discrimination risk. Role-counterfactual evidence can help show that those questions were measured rather than assumed away.

  • NIST AI Risk Management Framework and the AI RMF Core organize AI governance around Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage. ADFE fits most directly into Measure/Manage for role-specific political behavior.
  • The European Commission describes the EU AI Act as risk-based rules for AI developers and deployers. Its high-risk classification guidance underscores that intended purpose and deployment context matter.
  • U.S. agencies have emphasized that automated systems remain subject to existing civil-rights, consumer-protection, competition, credit, and employment laws; see the FTC-hosted joint statement on automated systems.
  • The EEOC AI resources highlight employment and disability-discrimination issues when software, algorithms, or AI are used to assess applicants and workers.
  • The CFPB circular on complex algorithms says creditors using AI/ML in credit decisions must still give specific principal reasons for adverse action.
  • The FTC's AI guidance and enforcement page frames deceptive claims, unfair practices, and discriminatory outcomes as live consumer-protection concerns.
