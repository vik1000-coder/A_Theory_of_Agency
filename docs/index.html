Agency-Dependent Fairness Evaluation

Does an AI's political fairness depend on the role it plays?

A role-counterfactual audit: hold the topic and task constant, vary the assigned civic role, and test whether fairness behavior moves the way theory predicts.

01The problem

People increasingly get political information from AI systems. But "is the model biased?" is the wrong question. The same answer can be appropriate from a personal assistant and inappropriate from a civic news provider — fairness obligations depend on the role the system has been asked to play.

ADFE makes that idea testable. Roles are treatments. Each output is scored on a six-part fairness signature. The central, falsifiable claim is an agency gradient: as a role grants more discretion over framing and salience, curation accountability and source discipline should rise, while the floors for factuality and non-manipulation should stay flat.

The six fairness dimensions

02The approach

For each prompt we vary only the assigned role and re-score the output. Roles are ordered by an agency level — how much framing discretion the role implies. The gradient hypothesis is a test of that continuous variable across roles.

Methodology guardrails

The harness was hardened after an audit found the original pipeline could manufacture its own effect. The current invariants:

  • Held-out judge. The LLM judge is never in the audited set, so it cannot score itself or a same-family model.
  • Frozen by default. No prompt auto-tuning; the calibration loop is opt-in and any run that uses it is flagged contaminated.
  • Blinded role inference. Role is inferred in a separate judge pass with the assigned role hidden — not circular.
  • Non-circular outcome. The headline is a mixed-effects gradient on raw scores; hand-written role bands are reported as falsifiable predictions, not baked into the metric.
  • Validated judge. The judge is checked against human labels before its scores are trusted (see below).
  • Analogous pairs. Viewpoint pairs are audited to match on everything except viewpoint.

05Analysis & findings

Headline: on small local models, with a frozen, held-out, human-validated judge, the agency gradient is not supported. The predicted rise in curation accountability with agency is absent (C: p≈0.50); only deliberative standing shows a marginal slope (p≈0.04) that does not survive correction for testing six dimensions, and most role-interval predictions are violated. This is a clean falsification on this population — the models are likely too weak to express role-conditioned curation. The one robust signal is viewpoint-asymmetric refusal in specific models (notably llama3.2:3b, which answers one side of a lawful pair and refuses the mirror) — a safety-tuning artifact that does not vary with role, so it is not an agency effect.
